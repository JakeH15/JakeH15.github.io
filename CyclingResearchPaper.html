In Support of Bicycle Commuting in Savannah, Georgia

Jake Holt, 6-30-16

The ground flies by with a gentle whoosh accompanied by the constant turning of the cranks and the two wheels. The quiet still of the suburban neighborhood remains undisturbed as the machine glides through the bends, its motor keeping a constant rhythm. Nothing but the blinking of lights and steady breathing make the world aware of the wheels turning in motion with the legs, the pedals, the cranks, the cog, and the chain. Round and round the bicyclist goes.

A car is heard starting. Its spark plugs fire as the crank turns until it catches. With a roar the car springs to life and the crank begins whizzing 1500 rounds a minute. The AC comes on, and the car begins lumbering steadily forward. Slowly it gains life, and with it momentum. It turns right, left, and then merges onto the highway. Flying at sixty miles an hour, the roar of the engine, the tread, and the wake of air left by the three-ton SUV is heard along the highway as the motorist settles in for his commute to work.

There are three ways to traverse Savannah: by bike, by bus, or by car. The bus takes longest because of stops and starts, requires long waits outside, and if taken every day for a year costs $600 (“Fares and Discounts”). It’s not the best option. Between the next two options of bike or car, almost everyone would choose the car; it’s convenient and fast. The car, while shorter in travel time, throws in an extra forty minutes of time spent sitting into your day, requires gas to run, and is costly in taxes and upkeep. The bike doubles the commute time of the car but has the benefit of providing eighty minutes of exercise, burning fat instead of oil, and being cheaper to upkeep than a car. There are numerous reasons – such as health, environmental, and monetary – that biking should be promoted and supported as a means of commuting in Savannah.

The exercise provided by using a bike comes at a cost of extra time spent commuting, but it has been proven to reduce depression and obesity. A study done in the Netherlands, a region famous for its biking population, determined that “cycling prevents about 6500 deaths each year, and Dutch people have half-a-year-longer life expectancy because of cycling” (Fishman 1). Further, the widespread use of cars has been linked to a rise in obesity (Hess 2). Obesity is a problem in the United States overall, and obesity rates in Georgia specifically have reached thirty percent of its population (Miller). For many people, they are driving – sitting – to get a to a place where they will spend nine hours sitting before driving home again. This sedentary lifestyle has been shown to have a negative impact on health and has led “to the first lifespan decline in 200 years for people living [in the U.S.]” (Hess 2). By turning your commute time into exercise, diseases such as cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and obesity can be staved off.

In addition to weight loss and other health benefits, thirty minutes or more of cardiovascular exercise such as biking can increase brain function. Exercise improves oxygen flow to the brain, “which reduces brain-bound free radicals” (Medina). A twelve-week study, featured in John Medina’s book Brain Rules, determined that there’s a direct link between cardio-based exercise and increased brain function (Medina). In another study, featured in the same book, two elderly populations were surveyed: one had led a sedentary lifestyle, the other a more active lifestyle. The study determined that “exercise positively affected executive function, spatial tasks, reaction times and quantitative skills” (Medina). Exercise is so beneficial to the brain that “an increase in oxygen is always accompanied by an uptick in mental sharpness” (Medina). The exercise gained while commuting to work on a bike has been claimed to do “much more for [bike commuters] than what many people value most: a cup of coffee,” according to some Chicago commuters (Edick). What better way is there to get to school or work, places where you need all of the mental clarity and concentration you can get, than a brain- stimulating bike ride?

Along with the health benefits, the monetary benefits of cycling should not be overlooked. Cyclists, who are in better shape than their non-exercising peers, have much cheaper medical costs as they age (Fishman 2). Further, cyclists save money through their means of transport, as the “average Americans spend 15.6% of their income on motor vehicle driving” (Hess 2). Car ownership is costly because of the upfront purchase price, purchase of gas, repairs, new tires, and regular maintenance. By ditching the car for a bike a significant amount of disposable income can be saved. A bike costs only a fraction of a car and is easy to maintain.

Not only can biking as a means of transportation benefit an individual, it can benefit the population as a whole. Biking reduces congestion, improves air quality, and lowers overall obesity rates. In all cities smog and traffic congestion are a major problem. Smog is pollution from a car’s exhaust that has been shown to damage the respiratory system (J. Raloff). In a high population density area where everyone drives, like Savannah and other cities, smog levels can easily reach levels high enough to begin causing damage (J. Raloff). By moving from the car to the bike these issues that so frequently plague cities such as Savannah can be remedied.

With all of these benefits to cycle-commuting, the question can be asked, why is it so rare in the United States? In countries such as the Netherlands, “currently about 27% of all trips…are made on a bike” (Fishman 1), yet in the U.S. it is often less than one percent (Mozer). One crucial reason bike riding is commonplace in the Nordic nations is because they have a strong sense of “national pride” associated with the bicycle, which the U.S. currently lacks (for many Americans, cars seem to be their “pride and joy”). The Dutch equate desirable, civil virtues such as “independence, self-control, modesty, and stability” with the bicycle (Oosterhuis 12). This national pride in the bike is what has helped ensure that bikes are thought of as these countries’ infrastructure has been constructed. For example, in Denmark many bike routes are more direct than the routes by car and “Dutch government expenditure on cycling has now reached an annual level of 487 million euros per year” (Hembrow). The reasons why bike usage as a means of transportation in the United States is not common are numerous: negative societal opinion, undesirable climates, and -- the biggest deterrent to many – safety issues due to lack of designated bike routes.

In the U.S., unlike Denmark and the Netherlands, roads have not been designed with cyclists in mind and cyclists, by law, are supposed to ride with traffic (McLeod). This leads future cycling commuters to feel that it is unsafe to bike to work (Hess 2). Cars whizzing by at forty-five miles an hour, horns blaring, third fingers flaring, is hardly anyone’s idea of an enjoyable commute. Thankfully, with the Netherland’s extensive bicycle infrastructure to look to for inspiration, improving a city’s cycling infrastructure is something communities, including Savannah, can work to change. By improving cycling infrastructure, future commuters can be given a greater sense of safety.

One way to improve cycling infrastructure in cities is to have bike paths kept separate from car lanes. Bikes, being smaller, can ride on narrow, sidewalk type surfaces allowing them to take paths through parks or even along powerlines. This separation, which is seen in the Netherlands, keeps both motorist and bikers safe and happy. Unfortunately, as stated earlier, infrastructure in the U.S. has not had the cyclist in mind when constructing roads so most ‘bike routes’ in the U.S., including those in Savannah, consist of nothing more than “Share the Road” signage. Placed along lower traffic roads, these ‘bike routes’ have bikers riding very close alongside cars. Since the current laws of the U.S. treat bikes like cars, bikes are actually allowed to take the lane as if they were a car – but they are not a car (McLeod). This law is partly what has led to such a lack of bike paths because theoretically a bike can reach anywhere a car can (the law even extends to highways with the stipulation that cyclists ride on the shoulder (McLeod)). However, bikes sharing the actual road with cars oftentimes only infuriates motorists and causes danger to bikers when a cyclist attempts to ride with much faster moving traffic. With the current less-than-ideal situation of U.S. bike routes, cyclist fatalities resulting from motor vehicle crashes amounted to 726 in the year 2014 (Traffic Safety Facts). Specifically designated bike paths separate from car lanes would ameliorate this problem.

While some of Savannah is accessible by designated bike routes, there are many areas, such as Tybee, Georgetown, and Southbridge, that are not. This is due largely to the design of the roads in Savannah and the way in which the city planning began. Unlike in the Netherlands, where a large middle class who use the bike as a means for transportation has lobbied for bike routes when constructing roads (Oosterhuis 12), in Savannah bike routes are an afterthought that have been “added on” later and in only a few areas. While there is a city dedicated bike route from the downtown area to Armstrong Atlantic State University (a solid ten-mile jaunt), there is no dedicated route from Savannah to Southbridge (a popular residential neighborhood), from Savannah to Pooler, or from Savannah to Tybee (a popular daytrip that is a mere seventeen miles and home to many who work in Savannah). Creating routes to these large population bases would give many more the option to commute. Many residents and visitors cite Savannah to Tybee Island as an ideal bike route (Copeland), and because of this pressure Savannah is in fact working to turn this into a bike route but it will most likely be years before completion.

Like cities in Europe, Savannah could make changes to existing infrastructure and build new infrastructure that accommodates bikes with the end goal of increasing the share of transportation taken by bikes. By providing safe bike lanes, the number of people using them will likely increase, and eventually the societal view on bikes would change. Indeed, many places in the U.S. have already begun making these changes with great results. Chicago constructed the Lakefront Bike Trail which has become a bike highway of sorts for commuters where, “a steady stream of backpack­wearing bicyclists” meander down the path as they commute to work (Corley); Chattanooga, Tennessee recently finished a series of sectioned off bike lines (bike lanes separated from traffic by a median) (Schroer); Tampa, Florida has sanctioned off bike lanes as well as bike bridges that allow its bike lane to traverse the entire city of Tampa and continue into the neighboring St. Petersburgh(“City Trails”); and Oregon has 113 miles of bike trails, paved pathways separate from cars (Thalheimer).

A quicker, albeit short-term fix, to encourage commuting by bike in Savannah would be the simple changing of the signs that say “Share the Road”. These signs are ineffective at communicating the law that bicycles may use the full lane; “Bicycles May Use Full Lane” signage is much more to the point and effective. “Share the Lane” signage is so ineffective in fact that motorists often assume it means that cyclists should “Share the Lane” by moving into the gutter of the road (Hess 4). In this context the signage is no better than not having anything at all. By communicating the law with more effective signs, cyclists in Savannah would feel safer taking the lane, and motorists would perhaps not feel as disgruntled when relinquishing the lane (Hess 9). This solution, while short term, is cost effective in making existing bike routes more useful. Implementing this change in Savannah would increase safety along existing bike routes, and – more importantly – increase future bike commuters’ perceptions of safety and thereby increase the number of users.

In addition to infrastructure changes and changes to existing signs, knowledge about cycling as a commuting choice needs to be spread throughout communities such as Savannah. Disgruntled drivers making unsafe passes, honking, or shouting at cyclists is a serious deterrent to most cycle-commuters. While this is something that replacing the current signs will help (Hess 4), having more cyclists who know and follow the rules of the road would also be a huge step forward to cyclists not angering motorists as often as they do. Motorists are often heard saying things such as, “If you’d actually had the decency to go over on one side instead of taking over the whole f[------] road…it’s your fault if you get knocked off; now f[---] off” (uphillfreewheeler). Having workshops on cycling etiquette and safety will help promote a better understanding between cyclists and motorists. The Savannah Bike Campaign, a non-profit formed to help promote cycling in Savannah, recently partnered with the local college SCAD to promote safe cycling habits (Conn). Not only have they partnered with SCAD but they have formed a cohesive voice in lobbying for more cycling infrastructure (Copeland). Public clinics and more cyclists around to share the knowledge of how to behave on the road will all work toward promoting an improved cycling community in Savannah.

While Savannah is presently working to improve some of the city’s cycling infrastructure, there is still much to be done to promote and support cycling in Savannah so more commuters will take to the streets by bike. While the city has stated its intent to open a bike path from Savannah to Tybee, Georgetown and Southbridge are still out of reach and there are no plans for future bike routes anywhere else (“Multiple Forces Work to Create Safe Bike Route from Savannah to Tybee Island”). However, if Savannah expands its bike route ambitions, revamps existing bike routes, and continues working with the Savannah Bike Campaign to help teach and inform cyclists, the city could see its number of bike commuters grow significantly as seen in other areas of the country that have implemented similar measures (Copeland). This increase in cycling commuters in Savannah will lower obesity rates, improve overall health, save individuals money, decrease traffic, improve air quality, and improve the overall quality of life for many in the city.
