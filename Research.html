February 18th, 2019

Snowflake Yeast, Settling Speed Selection Modes

For our second one week experiment, we once again used yeast that have the gene for cell wall cleavage during budding knocked out. We then created a procedure that mirrored the original procedure for evolving snowflake yeast, but with two different diameter settling tubes (10mm and 25mm). Our procedure can be seen here. We have yet to get data form particle image velocimetry, but just from viewing rough videos it is pretty clear that the 25mm tube has a different flow regime from the 10mm tube. Tung, a biomedical engineer on our research team, has designed a photo booth that we are going to 3d print this week. This should allow us to get images with a very clear seperation between the background and the particles (yeast). The clear seperation is important because seperating objects in a picture is pretty difficult task for computer programs to do.

Research Interests

I am interested in understanding the evolution of ('complex') life. I am currently working with the Snowflake yeast system to discover the modes and tempos at which multicellular life evolved. I have two projects in the works currently: what role do sedimentation regimes play in the Snowflake yeast system and under which conditions are non-clonal clusters preferred over conal clusters (the classic "coming together" vs "sticking together"). In addition to these projects that I am activley working on, I would like to model the growth period of Snowflake yeast as a markov chain (this ends up being a difficult graph theory problem) and I would like to apply Jonathan's work on hierarchial structures to evolution of hierarchial structures in biology. This would be a melding of Dan McShea's work with Jonathan's.
