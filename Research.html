February 18th, 2019

Snowflake Yeast, Settling Speed Selection Modes

For our second one week experiment, we once again used yeast that have the gene for cell wall cleavage during budding knocked out. We then created a procedure that mirrored the original procedure for evolving snowflake yeast, but with two different diameter settling tubes (10mm and 25mm). Our procedure can be seen here. We have yet to get data form particle image velocimetry, but just from viewing rough videos it is pretty clear that the 25mm tube has a different flow regime from the 10mm tube. Tung, a biomedical engineer on our research team, has designed a photo booth that we are going to 3d print this week. This should allow us to get images with a very clear seperation between the background and the particles (yeast). The clear seperation is important because seperating objects in a picture is pretty difficult task for computer programs to do.

February 10th, 2019

Snowflake Yeast, Settling Speed Selection Modes

This past week I was able to acquire the culture tubes, from Dr. Ratcliff's lab, needed to test run an experiment that we would like to carry out this semester. The plan is to look at how the diameter of the settling device affects the hydrodynamics of settling and the evolution snowflake yeast. Settling speed selection is a very accessible way to select for larger size. However, once the fitness gains from increased size start to drop off, other modes of improving fitness surface, such as better hydrodynamics from a change in body plan or individual cell shape (Ratcliff et al. 2013). Our experiment will look at how the settling tube's diameter impacts the flow regimes of the settling and what body plans and cell shapes differing diameters select for (if any at all).

  1. Ratcliff, Pentz, and Travisano (2013). Tempo and Mode of Multicellular Adaptation in Experimentally Evolved Saccharomyces Crevisiae. Evolution, 67-6: 1573-1581

February 2nd, 2019

Snowflake Yeast, Settling Speed Selection Modes

While waiting on materials, we've decided to run a one week trial experiment where we increase the viscocity of the settling medium used in the snowflake yeast settling speed procedure, which can be viewed here. To achieve the increased viscocity, we are using a 1:1 mixture of glycerol and growth media. For the yeast we are using snowflake yeast that were created by knocking out a gene responsible for cell wall cleavage during budding. From early videos, it appears that increasing the viscocity decreases the amount of updraft during settling; yeast that fall to the bottom stay at the bottom instead of being pushed back up. A full detail of the procedure can be found here. It also looks like the increased viscocity decreases the amount of turbulant flow, but this needs to be quantified.
