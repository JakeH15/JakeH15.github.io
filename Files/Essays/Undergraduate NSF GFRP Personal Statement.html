Undergraduate NSF GFRP Personal Statement

Jake Holt

September 2019

Personal, Background, and Future Goals Statement

My enthusiasm for exploring the natural world first manifested when I was young boy, at the age of five, on the coast of Maine. I would stay out all day and evening exploring the tidepools that dotted the coast, collecting crabs, urchins, and shells with my marine encyclopedia in hand, until the sun went down and my fingers grew numb from the frigid water. I enjoyed cataloging the organisms I discovered and observing their interactions, and I would return to the same tidepools day after day, looking for changes in the communities. By the time I retired that marine encyclopedia, at the age of ten, it was dog-eared and weather-worn.

In middle school, I began working in my dad’s workshop at home. My father, an aeronautical engineer, always encouraged me to dirty my hands and build things. He gave me an electronic circuit kit, taught me how to use machining tools and woodworking tools, how to weld, and how to use a 3D printer. He always supported my various projects (building a bass guitar body, constructing skateboard decks, and making my own guitar pickups) and encouraged me to ask questions and try new things. It was during this time, and thanks to him, that I developed an intuition for the physical world.

High school definitively made clear my attraction to science. It was in my science classes that I could connect my love of exploring the natural world with my love of creating things. From chemistry to biology to physics, I always enjoyed and thrived during lab activities. Most telling (given my current research with yeast) was my soda, kombucha, beer, and wine brewing activities. My freshman biology teacher and sophomore chemistry teacher unknowingly inspired me to begin making my own soda using yeast after covering aerobic and anaerobic respiration in their classes. I quickly moved from making soda to fermenting a number of different beverages, and the closet in my bedroom turned into an impromptu lab. I enjoyed playing with different strains, learning about biofilms through kombucha, and trying to keep populations healthy from one brew to the next. What started off innocently as a way to make my own soda, rapidly turned into a multi-year experiment in fermentation, one in which I documented different recipes and procedures.

During this same time period, I was exposed to tutoring and mentoring. Through my high school’s National Honor Society chapter, I tutored math and science at a local middle school, while at the same time taking over the coaching job at a local rock-climbing gym. I thrived as a coach and often found myself tutoring the kids after practice in math or science, telling them to bring their homework to the climbing gym. This reaching out to a younger generation through sport and tutoring is something I have continued throughout college (see Broader Impacts section).

My first exposure to academia came during my freshman year of college when I attended the 2016 Southeast Regional Math Conference as part of the Armstrong State University math club. At this conference, I attended a number of biophysics talks, which sparked my interest in studying biology with an emphasis on physics. The following year, my sophomore year, I transferred to the more rigorous Georgia Institute of Technology. After sophomore year at Tech, I joined Dr. Yunker’s (letter writer) Evolutionary Mechanics Vertically Integrated Projects (VIP) team. I have continued working with the team and I have expanded my involvement in Dr. Yunker’s lab to include an undergraduate thesis project (see Intellectual Merit section).

Through attending graduate school and studying the evolution of complex life in a multidisciplinary program, I plan on continuing to develop as an evolutionary biologist with the goals of teaching, conducting research at the intersection of biology, physics, and mathematics, and becoming a leader in my community through continued outreach and mentoring to the broader community. To achieve these goals, in graduate school I will gain experience from teaching assistantships, I will continue conducting research through experimental evolution projects, and I will improve and expand my knowledge base through coursework in mathematics, physics, and computational methods.

Intellectual Merit

While at Georgia Tech, starting sophomore year, I have completed senior level (mixed graduate and undergraduate) courses in biology (Experimental Design and Statistics, Protein Biology, Intro to Bioinformatics and Genomics, Evolution of Complex Life, and Molecular Evolution). In addition, I have signifigant research experience. My junior year, I joined Dr. Yunker’s (letter writer) Evolutionary Mechanics VIP team. The VIP program at Tech is focused on engaging undergraduates in research. My first semester with the team I chose to join the modeling sub-team because as a biologist on a multidisciplinary team I would have the opportunity for the most growth. Over the course of the semester, I learned to program in Processing and gained valuable insights from my more experienced team partners. After my initial semester on the VIP team, I chose to pursue an undergraduate thesis through Georgia Tech’s Undergraduate Research Opportunities Program. This program requires two technical writing classes, nine credit hours of independent research lab, and culminates with a thesis. I chose Dr. Yunker (physicist) and Dr. Ratcliff (letter writer and evolutionary biologist) as my co-mentors because I wanted to do an interdisciplinary project. The spring of my junior year, in addition to continuing to work with the VIP team and getting involved with the VIP Ambassador program, I began working independently in the lab on my thesis: Effect of Hydrodynamic Regime on Snowflake Yeast Evolution. During that semester, I developed an experimental evolution procedure, worked on modeling hydrodynamic regimes with Particle Image Velocimetry, used a Coulter Counter to take size distribution measurements, used ImageJ and an optical microscope to characterize yeast cells, and performed a trial evolution experiment using the developed procedure. As part of the VIP Team, I developed code in MATLAB and R to process microscopy data from experimental evolution projects.

During the same spring semester of my junior year, I presented a poster on my thesis research at both the Georgia Institute of Technology Undergraduate Research Symposium and the Southeastern Regional Yeast Meeting. At the Undergraduate Research Symposium, I gained valuable experience and feedback through creating a poster and presenting it to my peers. At the Southeastern Regional Yeast Meeting, I gave a strong presentation thanks to my prior experience, and I gained experience in the task of communicating my research to senior researchers outside of my field. At this meeting, I received valuable feedback on how to present my research to those outside of my field and I made connections with other yeast researchers. Also, I attended the Georgia Tech QBios Computational Workshop and the inaugural Evolution of Complex Life conference. At the QBios workshop, I expanded on my coding skills. At the Evolution of Complex Life conference, I presented a poster on my research and networked with other researchers in my field. I received valuable feedback on experimental design and methods, and I met potential collaborators at Emory University and University of Minnesota.

Last summer (the summer before my senior year), I received a research assistant position in Dr. Yunker’s lab. I carried out a long-term evolution experiment, coming into the lab seven days a week to perform transfers. I also took part in other projects: I helped an REU student with a bacterial evolution project, I learned how to use the confocal microscope and the light sheet microscope, I learned how to measure the elastic modulus of materials on the UTM, I wrote a stochastic simulation in Python to gain intuition on the snowflake yeast system, and I worked on taking microscope images for a statistical mechanics of living systems project, for which I developed a novel method of plating specimens.

This fall semester of my senior year, I have continued to work on my thesis and with the Evolutionary Mechanics VIP Team. My goal for my senior year is to successfully bring my thesis project to close by the time I graduate. Working on an ambitious, long term project like this one has taught me a number of valuable skills in planning and expectation management.

Broader Impacts

Through my involvement in sport, I have had the opportunity to mentor a younger generation and encourage their interest in science. When I left my job as the youth team coach of the climbing gym, I quickly got involved with a southeast cycling team, Welland Racing U23 & Junior Development Team. Welland took me on as a fully supported U23 rider with the expectation that I would not only train and race with the goal of being nationally competitive, but that I would also act as a role model and mentor for their younger riders. Through this role, I have continued mentoring young athletes, just as I did in the climbing gym. I actively advise younger riders on applying to college, help with science fair projects, and tutor when needed in math and science. I am set to graduate out of the U23 program at the end of 2019, but I will continue to be involved with the program as a mentor, and I will continue to use this mentorship opportunity to foster an interest in science in the younger members. More recently, I have become involved with an Atlanta-based junior cycling development team, where I act in a similar role as I have to the Welland team. I give junior riders tours of Georgia Tech, offer tutoring services, and actively helped them with engineering and science projects. In addition to mentoring, I have competed regionally in professional track cycling races for Welland Racing.

During my time at Georgia Tech, I have been actively involved in the leadership of the Georgia Tech Cycling club, where I have held a number of different roles. I took on each role with a specific objective and learning goal in mind, and each time I accomplished both, honing my skill in community outreach and management. As New Member Coordinator during my sophomore year, I successfully grew the size of the club by twenty percent. The following year, as the Vice President of Road Cycling, I developed incoming talent, planned the 2019 road season, organized and hosted a profitable, two-day race event, and led the team to our 2019 conference championship win. This year as the team secretary, I have vigorously renewed our existing corporate sponsorship relations, brought on a new corporate sponsor, and worked behind the scenes to organize the team documents so that future officers have a clear procedural list to follow and we are not in danger of losing our non-profit status. In addition to holding these leadership positions, I have trained rigorously to become a competitive cyclist at a national level, and I have successfully represented Georgia Tech at multiple national championship events (2018 and 2019 USAC Track Nationals; 2019 USAC Road Nationals).

While visiting Detroit last winter for a cycling race, I was inspired by the Detroit Boxing Gym Youth Program, where the slogan is “Books before Boxing”. The program focuses on math and literature development and college preparation for youths in the Detroit area, while also supporting their development as boxers. I plan to take what I have learned from my roles as a rock-climbing coach, peer mentor, VIP Ambassador, and a leader of the Georgia Tech Cycling Club to start a similar youth outreach program after graduation, with a focus on scientific education, academic success, and character building through sport. My past mentoring experiences, my success as a cyclist and rock-climber, my involvement with the VIP Ambassador program, and my planned career as a scientist make me uniquely suited to achieve this vision.
