Paper Critique of Shajani-Yi, Z., Frequency of Somatic TP53 Mutations in Combination with Known Pathogenic Mutations... Neoplasia (New York, N.Y.), 20(3), 256-262.

Jake Holt

This paper looks at occurrence of somatic TP53 mutations, the proportion of individual TP53 mutations, and the co-occurrence of actionable genes (BRAF, EGFR, KRAS, PIK3CA, and IDH1) in Gliomas, Colon Adenocarcinoma, and Non-Small Cell Lung Carcinoma. The research hypothesized that looking at the individual TP53 mutations and co-occurring actionable genes, target for therapeutic drugs could be found (1). Mutations to the TP53 gene occur more commonly than to any other gene in human cancer. TP53 encodes a transcription factor, p53, and is located on chromosome 17p13, composed of 11 exons, and 19,149 base pairs (NM_000546.5). The p53 protein acts as a tumor suppressor and regulates a variety of cellular functions, such as: metabolism, cell cycle arrest, DNA repair, senescence, and apoptosis. The mutations associated with TP53 and human cancer lead to a loss of function, or a loss of the ability to suppress tumors (2-4). Further, mutated TP53 interacts and upregulates different sets of genes than the wild type (12). The most common TFP3 mutations, also known as hot spot mutations, have oncogenic properties (metastasis, cell survival, proliferation, and invasion are promoted by these mutations (5,6)). While gene therapy can be used to restore function to mutated TP53, that technology is still deemed very dangerous and has not been successful thus far (7). In contrast, oncogenes can be treated therapeutically as actionable genes, and this is largely what drove the investigators.

To achieve this, the researchers used de-identified patient samples taken from three common cancers located in different regions of the body. The samples were “formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) surgical and cytology tumor samples” (1, pg. 257). Of the samples, only those with percent cellularity and tumor content greater than ten percent were used. The work flow was as follows: “DNA was extracted using the Gentra Pure Gene Kit (Qiagen) (2013-August 2015) and QIAmp DNA FFPE Kit (Qiagen) (August 2015-June 2016). DNA quantification and DNA quality were performed using the Quant-iT PicoGreen dsDNA Assay Kit (Invitrogen) and KAPA hgDNA Quantification and QC Kit (KAPA Biosystem, respectively…Library preparation was performed using the Ion AmpliSeq Cancer Hotspot Panel v2 (CHPv2) (Life Technology) as described in previous studies (8,9). Libraries were quantidied, normalized, pooled, and sequenced using the Ion 318 Chip v2 or Ion 316 Chip v2 on the PGM Sequencing Platform. Variants were identified using the Variant Caller Plugin (v4.0.2) available in the Torrent Suite and Golden Helix SVS (v7.7.8) was used to assess quality and functional predictions.” (1, pg. 257). Only patients diagnosed with the listed cancers and positive for TP53 mutations were selected.
